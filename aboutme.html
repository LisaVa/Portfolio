About me

Hi I’m Lisa, a UX designer deeply passionate about researching and designing logical, intuitive and scalable products. You can read through this section to get to know a little bit more about me and my career, or you can skip ahead to my skills, tools I’ve worked with, or my resume. Thanks for reading!

Summary

I am a UX Designer with a background in program and process management, content writing, and stakeholder engagement. After graduating in 2013 I spent three and a half years working at Business Talent Group (BTG), where I began in sales but quickly switched to a role on the Client Service team that gave plenty of scope to my people-oriented approach, strategic organization, and creativity. As a designer, I leverage these skills to understand how users expect to engage with products and designing intuitive processes to to meet these expectations. I am currently completing a UX Design Apprenticeship through Bloc, and am eager to join a dynamic UX team where my skills can shine.

Career Summary

Career

I graduated from Barnard College in 2013 with a degree in History, and immediately joined an exciting company called Business Talent Group (BTG). BTG was one of the earliest professional services firms to capitalize on the growing gig-economy, and their business model is centered on enabling global companies to engage independent consultants or experts for project-based work. I ended up spending three and a half years at this amazing company, beginning in sales but quickly moving over to client service and program management. Through this experience, I learned that professionally my strongest skills and the roles I am most drawn to are people-oriented, highly organized and efficiency-driven, and creative.

People-oriented

I had initially pursued sales as a career because I am a highly extroverted person and enjoy working with people to understand their goals. However, I rapidly realized that the frontline sales is fundamentally a volume game, and that while I might get to reach out to many people on any given day, conversations and exchanges were going be brief and not give that much scope the relationship-building or problem solving I craved.

Fortunately for me, my manager helped me identify that with my writing skills and organization I was a much stronger fit for our Client Service team. At BTG, Client service is responsible for working with clients from the moment they have a specific project for a consultant through the consultant’s last day. This gave plenty of scope for my people-skills; I got to work clients on a longer-term basis to understand their goals, and became highly skilled in identifying these, both the explicit (e.g. “find me a consultant who knows supply-chain”) and the unstated (“Also this person needs to be a good coach who won’t terrify my new, inexperienced right-hand support but rather help them build the skills they need”).

I became particularly skilled at the latter, and was generally able to quickly identify clients who knew exactly what they want from clients who needed a bit more help scoping, to clients who had reached out to use on a trial basis or to satisfy an internal vendor outreach quota and weren’t likely to actually engage.

Strategically Organized

The Client Service role also capitalized on my natural organization and efficiency. The life-cycle for projects has many steps: after the initial client interview we identified consultants from our n etwork who look like a promising fit, screened them, wrote targeted bios of them, worked with the final candidate(s) on a formal proposal, handled all the project contracting and coordinating once it was greenlit, and then stay in touch with both consultant(s) and client(s) throughout the project to ensure its success. There were many details to keep track of and forgetting any of them could derail a project. I am by nature someone who not only understands how to implement processes, but am also able to understand the reason why behind them, and before long I was not only navigating a high volume of projects, I was also better ways to organize myself. I would create dashboards in salesforce to help me track progress at across the board, lists of go-to consultants for specific roles or clients, and reusable templates for specific proposal types.

Before long I was not only managing a higher volume of proposals than the average associate, I was also responsible for helping review the operations of the whole team. I would monitor team dashboards, and reach out to associates on teams that seemed stuck on a particular step of the process, or remind them to update steps we needed for compliance. This also gave my people-skills a chance to shine--it’s very tough to coach your peers without causing offence, and finding the best way to do this took some thought.

Creativity

My people skills and organization meant that I accelerated rapidly in the Client Service role; within a year of joining the team I was promoted to Senior Associate, and a year after that I was a Program Manager formally spearheading my own projects and managing an Associate. However, my last phase at BTG allowed my creativity to shine through an increasing responsibility designing and writing proposals.

Throughout the client service role I had drawn praise for my writing skills; as an associate I wrote great, fast consultant bios, and as a Program Manager I was able continue to hone my writing and editing skills putting together formal proposals, often working with consultants with decades more career experience than myself to help them appeal to particular clients or position their work. This included designing visually appealing powerpoints, graphs and templates, which I hugely enjoyed. By the end of 2016 as a program manager I was increasingly recruited by senior leadership to work with teams handling especially complex RFPs, proposals, or client life-cycles, and this was by far and away my favorite aspect of my job during this time.

When I was promoted to Senior Program Manager in January 2017, it was with the understanding that I was going to formally work on the BTG proposal response function, specifically around RFP’s (requests for proposals) and help create reusable, dynamic templates to help us respond to these more efficiently and more appealingly. I threw myself into adapting my recent proposal efforts into broader templates, and writing original case studies that could be pulled quickly into proposals, and by the time I left BTG six months later we were well on our way to a more robust process.

Finding UX

Even while working on this exciting new project, I knew that ultimately the time was coming for the next phase of my career. While the roles at BTG had offered plenty of scope for my skill to join, I knew long term I wanted to find something different for myself. I wals always jealous of the consultants I placed on projects, who often got to build something lasting and long term at their various clients. While I loved working with people, I wanted to build things too.

I also had a lifetime interest in technology, which I had relatively limited scope to utilize in my role at BTG. However in college I had had an internship where I was responsible for spearheading the redesign of a website, and I had enjoyed interviewing stakeholders, analyzing user surveys, and sketching wireframes. In hindsight I realized that had been one of the most enjoyable projects of my short career, and realized that approaching it again with my experience at BTG would make me highly skilled at it. My people-oriented background would inform user research and interviews, the strategic organization as a program manager would help build-in logical steps and processes in my products, and the creativity as a writer and proposal designer would shine in wireframes and visual design.

However, I knew I would need a grounding in UX principles before I found a role, so after looking at various graduate school programs and bootcamps I decided to enroll in Bloc’s UX Design Apprenticeship. Bloc offers an immersive User Experience Design program that focuses on User Experience, Visual Design, and Coding fundamentals (HTML, CSS, Javascript). The selling point for me was that students are paired with a professional UX Designer who coaches them 1:1 multiple times a week, providing informed and personalized feedback on their work and progress. Given my extroverted nature I had initially shied away from online programs, but this level of individual attention was incredible, and sold me on Bloc.

And I’m very glad it did, because thanks to a thoughtful curriculum and a highly experienced mentor I feel prepared and ready to take on my first UX role, utilizing the skills I’ve developed over my career and at Bloc, and the toolsets I’m knowledgeable in.
