Gowork

Designing and prototyping a location-based app that users to identify and compare coworking spaces in New York City


4 weeks | Concept Project

Research | Information Architecture | Visual Design | Prototyping

Background

Though the number of coworking spaces in New York City has grown exponentially in the past few years, there are surprisingly few resources to help potential users find the right coworking space for them. Freelancers and other potential coworkers may use standard crowd-sourced solutions such as Yelp, but these are not tailored to screen for specific worker needs. Similarly, while larger coworking solutions such as WeWork offer maps to identify their locations, this naturally limits the user to only one coworking option.


A map of coworking spaces in NYC, from The New Worker.


Given the success of platforms that help locate and compare space options in numerous other markets (for instance, Priceline in the travel market, Seamless in the restaurant market) I believed that offering a similar solution in the Coworking space will offer a needed solution to thousands of NYC based users.

Problem

Given the obvious lack of resources for people who wanted to find a coworking space and didn’t want to wade through countless returns on google search, I set out to build an app that solved the following user problems:

Users want to find coworking spaces that are geographically convenient

Users want to view the details of the space and easily compare it to other spaces

Users want to easily save and share the coworking spaces they find

Users want read reviews of these spaces, and write their own reviews after using them

Solution

This app represented the solution to a two sided problem. It needed both to represent what the coworking industry currently offers freelance/independent New Yorkers, and which of these many features people considered most important when choosing a workspace. With Gowork, users are able to easily search by location, filter by price and features, and read what other users have to say about their experience at locations. Moreover, with an account they can save spaces they are interested in, and write their own reviews to contribute to the broader community.

Survey

After conducting my secondary research, I had enough information to build a framework for my primary research. I was well versed in what features coworking spaces offered, where they were located, and the multitude of pricing plans they leveraged, but I did not know how users prioritized this. Before I built my app, I needed to know what users most wanted to know about coworking. Accordingly, I built a survey to answer the following questions:

  1. How interested are people in coworking, and how frequently would they utilize a coworking space?
  2. What features are most important when people choose a coworking space?
  3. What amenities would they most like to have in a coworking space?
  4. How likely would they be to use a coworking space to work with others?

Survey Results

  1. How interested are people in coworking, and how frequently would they utilize a coworking space?
    • 75% interested in a coworking space
    • 75% would ideally use more than once a week
    • 5% thought they would need a coworking space on short notice
  2. What features are most important when people choose a coworking space?

    Users were asked to prioritize a list of features from most to least important. The higher the score of the feature, the more people had prioritized highly. The results were as follows:

    • Pricing (96)
    • Location (85)
    • Having a designated/reserved sitting area (66)
    • User reviews (50)
    • Available amenities (50)
    • Accessibility outside business hours (48)
  3. What amenities would they most like to have in a coworking space?

    Users were asked to prioritize a list of amenities from most to least important. The higher the score of the amenity, the more people had prioritized highly. The results were as follows:

    • Printing/scanning services (88)
    • Available refreshments (70)
    • Presentation and meeting rooms (68)
    • Space to collaborate with colleagues (63)
    • Social/community events (e.g. happy hours, networking opportunities, brown-bag lunches) (58)
    • Package and mail delivery services (49)
  4. How likely would they be to use a coworking space to work with others?
    • While the majority expressed interest in using a coworking space to collaborate with colleagues, they generally were not strongly interested (somewhat interested as opposed to interested).
    • Similarly, the while there was interest in using coworking space to meet with clients, the majority who expressed this interest only described themselves as being “somewhat interested.""

User Stories

The initial list of User Stories took into account all my findings from my primary and secondary research

I then used the survey results to prioritize features for the initial MVP. In the example below, I prioritized filters that users should have to narrow their search results. The items highlighted in red are the most critical, and were included in this MVP. Yellow highlighted items are secondary features, and the blue are the least critical.

Prioritized user stories.

I prioritized the two most popular amenities (Printing services and available beverages) and paired them with reserved seating for our initial amenities features. I chose to move reserved seating from a feature to an amenity to condense the number of filters in the search functionality of the first version of the app, and because on further review it seemed to fit better with amenities. The other features have a universal aspect (hours, pricing, location) that could easily be found in any industry, but reserve seating is more specific, and in hindsight fit better with amenities.


Accessibility outside business hours: While this scored relatively low overall on features, I opted to keep in the MVP for 3 reasons:

  1. A significant minority ranked it as their number 1 consideration (around 10% of respondents).
  2. My benchmark study of apps in other verticals indicated hours of operation is a standard feature, leading to the concern users would expect to see it
  3. Research into the freelance/independent workforce indicated that many work irregular hours or are freelancing part time/on top of daytime commitments, leading me to believe that despite the survey results a significant number of users might find this useful (in my user personas, both the entrepreneur and the writer would potentially benefit from this feature)

Spotlight on pricing

Survey results indicated that pricing was by far the most important consideration for users when choosing a Coworking space. However, pricing plans vary across coworking spaces, often depending on how often a user will use the space, whether they are reserving a seat, and whether they are signing up on a short-term or long term basis.

My initial solution was to look at the relative overall prices of each coworking space and use a scale similar to Yelp’s dollar sign feature to help users indicate whether they were looking on the lower end or the higher end of the pricing spectrum:

However, initial user feedback immediately made it clear that this would not work. While people have a rough understanding of what a $ meal is versus a $$$$ meal (McDonalds versus Per Se) there was nothing like the same general knowledge about coworking space pricing. I had to find a way to give people numbers they could filter by. I went back to the pricing breakdown above, and looked for commonalities. Eventually, I concluded that since all the spaces offered a daily and monthly pricing plan I could utilize this to give more targeted ranges. I went through and looked at the different daily and monthly offerings on each of these spaces to come up with these broad ranges:

  • Daily Pricing: $20 - $150
  • Monthly Pricing: $80 - $1,000

Users had the ability to choose whether they were looking for a daily or monthly price option when filtering, and immediately got feedback on what the price ranges would be. The response was much more positive--users wanted to have a sense of actual dollar costs, and while these ranges were broad they were effective in immediately anchoring them in what prices were likely to be, and how to think about their search.

Visual Design

Branding

The name: After many iterations, I elected to use the name Gowork for this app. It was a nice visual play on “Cowork”, and the imperative of the “go” captured and underlined that this was an app to enable people to leave their homes to go work.



Color scheme: Blue is famously associated with business, and I chose a lighter, brighter blue to underscore that this app enables a new, modern way of getting business done. I added a complimentary teal as an accent and a strong contrasting red to be a call-to-action.

Typeface: I chose Rubik because as a san-serif with it’s rounded corners it was highly readable even on a smaller screen, which is the primary screen people were going to use to view this app. It offers 5 weight styles which made it easy to create a visually distinctive type hierarchy (see below image):

Prototype

As a final deliverable, I built a prototype of the app using Flinto. Using Flinto allowed me to design interactions for users as they went selected different screens and features.

I asked users to complete the following tasks

  1. Create an account
  2. Search for a coworking space
  3. Favorite their coworking space
  4. Write a review of the space
  5. Delete their review

Results showed that users were able to complete the assigned tasks without confusion

Reflection

Reflection is key

The research I did into the coworking industry in New York and to benchmark apps in other verticals helped enormously is guiding this project. This baseline understanding allowed me to know exactly what gaps in my knowledge I needed users to fill, and which amenities and features I needed them to prioritize. Accordingly, when it came time to complete the information architecture I felt confident making decisions about information hierarchy and prioritization.

More user feedback

While I got some great feedback from my peers and my mentor on the earlier stages of the design, I would have liked to have widened the group giving feedback on the final prototype. User feedback was key to helping me re-approach the pricing filter, among other decisions. Given time constraints I did not have time to recruit many people who were entirely new to the project, and I would have liked to see less experienced reactions to it.

Overall

This project represents a strong first version of an app that would have great potential real world use.If this were not a school project I would be eager to see how it fared once it was released. When prioritizing user stories for the MVP I discarded many that I believe would ultimately make it into a later version of this app. I could foresee this evolving, in potentially with coworking spaces, and incorporating the ability to filter for a broader range of filters, or provide reviews on specific aspects of a coworking space. There are many ways this could develop.
